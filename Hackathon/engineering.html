Only fifteen percent of positions in the engineering field are held by women. Let's change that

Challenges faced by Women in Math:

  • Educational barriers
  • Lack of representation in leadership roles
  • Salary disparities
  • Unconcious bias in hiring and promoting
  • Hostile work environments

Before the unfortunate COVID-19 pandemic, the number of female engineers was steadily growing, reaching a peak of 14%. However, in the wake of the pandemic, women in this field have suffered a great setback, bringing their numbers down to around 13%. Steps need to be taken to change this!

In the dynamic world of engineering, female engineers encounter a unique set of challenges that shape their professional journeys. From navigating male-dominated work environments to confronting gender bias in project assignments and performance evaluations, women engineers often face uphill battles in their quest for recognition and advancement. The scarcity of female role models and mentors further compounds these challenges, leaving many women engineers to navigate the complexities of the field on their own. Despite these obstacles, female engineers continue to defy stereotypes and push boundaries, contributing their innovative perspectives and technical expertise to tackle some of society's most pressing challenges. As the engineering profession strives for greater diversity and inclusion, it is imperative that we address systemic barriers and create supportive environments where women engineers can thrive and lead with confidence.

Overcoming these barriers is no easy task...however, there are many women who have overcome these challenges and risen to greatness!

Let's celebrate these high-reaching modern female engineers

Gwynne Shotwell is the COO and President of SpaceX. Under her leadership, SpaceX was the first private company to send a satellite into geostationary orbit, too. Setting new standards is one of her favorite things about the job, with milestones like landing a first-stage booster on a drone ship and on land, re-flying a rocket, launching Falcon Heavy, the most powerful rocket currently in operation.

Alicia Boler Davis is leading GM.'s global manufacturing operations and its manufacturing, engineering and labor-relations organization. She is a member of the GM senior leadership team and reports to GM CEO and Chairman Mary Barra.

These women are an inspiration, and can be used as role models for generations of female engineers to come!
